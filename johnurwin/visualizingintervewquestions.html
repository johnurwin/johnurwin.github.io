Interview Questions for DCF Model

Basic Question 1: What is the purpose of a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model?

A DCF model estimates the value of an investment based on its expected future cash flows, discounted back to their present value. It helps assess whether an investment is undervalued or overvalued.

Basic Question 2: What is the significance of the discount rate in a DCF model?

The discount rate reflects the risk and time value of money. It adjusts future cash flows to their present value and is typically the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) in most DCF calculations.

Medium Question 1: How does changing the terminal growth rate affect the terminal value and overall present value?

An increase in the terminal growth rate raises the terminal value, as it assumes higher perpetual growth. This, in turn, increases the overall present value. Conversely, lowering the terminal growth rate reduces the terminal value and present value.

Medium Question 2: What happens if the WACC is higher than the terminal growth rate in the model?

If the WACC is higher than the terminal growth rate, the terminal value remains positive and realistic. However, if the WACC is close to or less than the terminal growth rate, the model may produce an unrealistically high or infinite terminal value.

Advanced Question 1: Explain how you would validate the assumptions in the DCF model to ensure reliability.

- Validate revenue growth rates and operating margins using historical data and industry benchmarks.
- Cross-check the WACC calculation against market data for equity and debt costs.
- Stress-test the model by varying key inputs (e.g., growth rates, WACC) and evaluating the impact on present value.
- Ensure the terminal growth rate aligns with the long-term expected growth of the economy or industry.

Advanced Question 2: What are the limitations of the DCF model, and how would you address them in decision-making?

Limitations include sensitivity to assumptions (e.g., discount rate, growth rate), inability to account for qualitative factors, and potential over-reliance on future projections.
To address these, perform scenario analysis, incorporate additional valuation methods (e.g., comparable company analysis), and include qualitative factors in decision-making.

What is Cross-Validation?

Cross-validation is a method to evaluate the performance of machine learning models by splitting the data into training and testing sets multiple times. This ensures that the model is trained and tested on different portions of the data, reducing the risk of overfitting and giving a better estimate of model performance.

In K-Fold Cross-Validation, the data is divided into K subsets (folds). Each fold is used as a testing set once, while the remaining folds are used as the training set. The process is repeated K times, and the final performance is averaged across all iterations.

Questions and Answers about K-Means and DBSCAN

Basic Question 1: What is the main difference between K-Means and DBSCAN?

K-Means assigns points to a fixed number of clusters based on distance to centroids, while DBSCAN groups points based on density and can identify noise (outliers).

Basic Question 2: What happens if K-Means is run on data with non-spherical clusters?

K-Means may fail to group points correctly because it assumes clusters are spherical and evenly sized.

Intermediate Question 1: How does DBSCAN decide which points are noise?

DBSCAN identifies points with fewer than a specified number of neighbors (MinPts) within a given radius (Epsilon) as noise.

Intermediate Question 2: What is a key challenge when choosing the number of clusters for K-Means?

The number of clusters (K) must be specified beforehand. Choosing the wrong K can lead to poorly formed clusters.

Advanced Question 1: Can DBSCAN produce overlapping clusters like K-Means?

No, DBSCAN does not produce overlapping clusters. Each point belongs to one cluster or is labeled as noise.

Advanced Question 2: What happens if DBSCAN's Epsilon (radius) value is set too small or too large?

If Epsilon is too small, many points will be labeled as noise. If too large, clusters may merge, losing meaningful separation.

Out-of-the-Box Question 1: How can K-Means and DBSCAN be used together to detect meaningful clusters and noise?

K-Means can first identify the general structure of clusters, and DBSCAN can be applied afterward to detect dense regions and label remaining points as noise.

Out-of-the-Box Question 2: What could you learn by applying DBSCAN to the output of K-Means clustering?

Applying DBSCAN on K-Means results can highlight regions where clusters overlap or detect points at the cluster boundaries as noise.
